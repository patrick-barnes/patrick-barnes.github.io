# Explorer Career Structure

## Overview

Many highly curious technologists eventually discover that a single job rarely satisfies a 70% Explorer preference. The roles that come closest often combine two complementary activities—one that provides stability and credibility, and another that maximizes exploration.

Think of it as an Explorer career structure rather than a single job title.

## A Common “Explorer Lifestyle” Structure

1. The Anchor Role (Income + Credibility)

This is your main professional role—often something like:

- Principal Engineer
- Technology Strategist
- Innovation Architect
- CTO Office Technologist

In these positions, you still explore emerging technologies but also apply your experience to guide strategy or architecture.

Companies that frequently have such roles include:

- Microsoft
- Amazon
- Google

Typical time allocation in the anchor role:

- ~40–60% exploring new technologies
- ~40–60% advising, synthesizing, or building

This role gives you:

- stable income
- credibility
- access to new technologies

2. The Exploration Channel (Ideas + Learning)

Alongside the anchor role, many explorers maintain a channel where they share what they’re discovering.

Examples:

- writing essays or research notes
- publishing a newsletter
- making educational videos
- speaking at conferences

Platforms that make this easier today include:

- Substack
- YouTube

Benefits:

- forces you to explore many domains
- builds reputation as a thinker
- connects you with interesting people


3. The Network Role (Exposure to New Ideas)

Explorers often cultivate roles that expose them to new ideas and industries.

Examples include:

- startup advisor
- venture scout
- open-source project collaborator

Organizations that use this model include venture ecosystems such as:

- Andreessen Horowitz
- Y Combinator

This role increases the variety of problems you encounter.

## What This Career Structure Looks Like

Many curious technologists end up with a career that roughly looks like:

| Activity | Percentage |
| -------- | ---------- |
| Anchor job | 50–70% |
| Exploration / research | 15–25% |
| Publishing / teaching | 10–20% |
| Advising / networking | 5–15% |

That combination often produces something very close to a 70% Explorer experience.

## Why This Works

Explorers often enjoy learning, connecting ideas, and sharing insights, but a single job rarely includes all three in the right balance.

Combining roles allows you to:

- explore widely
- maintain professional credibility
- influence interesting decisions

Many modern technologists operate this way without formally labeling it.
